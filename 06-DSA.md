# The EU Digital Services Act (DSA) — Regulation (EU) 2022/2065

## 1. Overview

The Digital Services Act (DSA) is a comprehensive, regulatory framework that redefines the responsibilities of online intermediaries operating in the European single market. Applicable since February 17, 2024, the DSA modernizes the decades-old eCommerce Directive (Directive 2000/31/EC). It establishes a strict, asymmetric legal framework for online platforms, proportional to their size and impact on society, to ensure a safe, predictable, and trusted online environment.

The primary ethical objective of the DSA is to protect fundamental human rights (such as freedom of expression, non-discrimination, and child safety) in the digital sphere, tackling illegal content, systemic disinformation, and algorithmic manipulation.

## 2. Asymmetric Scope of Application

The DSA recognizes that different platforms pose different levels of risk to society. It categorizes services into four tiers, with obligations accumulating as scale increases.

1. **Intermediary Services** (e.g., ISPs, DNS providers): Basic transparency obligations.
2. **Hosting Services** (e.g., Cloud providers, web hosting): Notice and action mechanisms, plus user-notification obligations.
3. **Online Platforms** (e.g., social networks, app stores, collaborative economy platforms): Complaint systems, trusted flaggers, ban on dark patterns, ad transparency, and parental controls.
4. **Very Large Online Platforms** (VLOPs) & **Very Large Online Search Engines** (VLOSEs): Platforms with over 45 million active monthly users in the EU (representing 10% of the EU population). They face the most stringent obligations due to their systemic impact on public discourse and democratic processes.

## 3. Core Provisions & Obligations

### 3.1. "What is Illegal Offline is Illegal Online"
- **Notice and Action** (Article 16): Hosting providers and platforms must implement easy-to-use, standardized mechanisms allowing users to report illegal content. Upon receiving a notice, platforms must act "expeditiously" to remove or disable access to the illegal content.
- **Statement of Reasons** (Article 17): If a platform decides to remove content, restrict visibility, or suspend an account, it must provide a clear and detailed "statement of reasons" to the affected user, explaining the exact policy violated and the mechanism used (automated or human).


### 3.2. Algorithmic Transparency & Recommendations (Articles 27 & 38)
- **Recommender systems:** Platforms must explain in their terms and conditions the main parameters used in their algorithmic recommendation systems and how users can modify or influence those parameters.
- **Non-Profiling Option:** VLOPs and VLOSEs must provide at least one recommendation system option that is not based on profiling (e.g., a purely chronological feed).


### 3.3. Elimination of Dark Patterns (Article 25)

- The DSA strictly prohibits online platforms from designing, organizing, or operating their user interfaces in a way that deceives, manipulates, or materially distorts the ability of users to make free and informed decisions.
- Examples of banned practices include making cancellation processes artificially difficult or repeatedly prompting users to accept tracking after they have declined.

### 3.4. Advertising Transparency & Targeting Restrictions (Articles 26 & 28)

- **Ad Disclosure:** Users must be able to identify in real-time that an item is an advertisement, who paid for it, and the parameters used to target them.
- **Banning Sensitive Profiling:** Platforms are prohibited from presenting ads targeted using personal data categorized as sensitive under the GDPR (e.g., sexual orientation, religious beliefs, political affiliations, health data).
- **Minor Protection:** Ad targeting based on profiling is completely prohibited when the platform knows with reasonable certainty that the user is a minor.


## 4. Special Mandates for VLOPs and VLOSEs

Due to their power, VLOPs (e.g., TikTok, Instagram, YouTube, X) must comply with systemic risk-mitigation duties:

- **Annual Risk Assessments** (Article 34): They must systematically analyze the risks stemming from their designs, including dissemination of illegal content, negative effects on fundamental rights, manipulation of public debate (disinformation), gender-based violence, and protection of minors.
- **Independent Audits** (Article 37): Subject to independent, external audits at their own expense once a year to verify compliance with the DSA obligations.
- **Data Access for Researchers** (Article 40): Must grant vetted academic researchers access to real-time public data and API endpoints to monitor systemic risks and algorithm behavior.

## 5. Governance and Penalties

- **Digital Services Coordinators (DSC):** Each Member State designates a national DSC (the Comisión Nacional de los Mercados y la Competencia - CNMC in Spain) to monitor compliance.
- **Fines:** Fines are extraordinarily high for non-compliance, scaling up to 6% of the global annual turnover of the provider in the preceding financial year.

## 6. Implications for Digital Ethics Practitioners

1. **De-biasing Recommender Engines:** Designing and evaluating ethical recommender systems that prioritize organic engagement and safety over extreme emotional polarization (clickbait/hate speech).
2. **Dark Pattern Prevention:** Auditing UX/UI designs to ensure user autonomy is respected, making consent and privacy settings easily navigable.
3. **Risk Mitigation Pipelines:** Establishing standard operating procedures for continuous "systemic risk assessments" in digital products, looking at how product features might be weaponized for coordinated disinformation campaigns.
