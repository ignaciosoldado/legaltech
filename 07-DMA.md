# The EU Digital Markets Act (DMA) — Regulation (EU) 2022/1925

## 1. Executive Summary & Overview

The Digital Markets Act (DMA) is a sector-specific antitrust regulation designed to ensure contestable and fair markets in the digital sector across the European Union. Applicable since May 2023, the DMA shifts the enforcement model from slow, ex-post competition investigations to a strict, ex-ante compliance framework.

This process may often take years to conclude, while market-dominant players wipe out rivals. Because of that, instead of waiting for an abuse of power to happen, the DMA pre-emptively designates massive tech platforms as "Gatekeepers" and binds them to a list of explicit "do's and don'ts" to prevent anticompetitive behaviors before they occur.

## 2. Who are the "Gatekeepers"? (Article 3)

A company is designated as a "Gatekeeper" if it provides a Core Platform Service (such as search engines, social networks, operating systems, browsers, messaging apps, cloud computing, or online marketplaces) and meets the following quantitative thresholds:

**GATEKEEPER DESIGNATION THRESHOLDS**
1. **Annual EU Turnover:** ≥ €7.5 Billion (or market cap ≥ €75 Billion)
2. **Active Users in EU:** ≥ 45 Million monthly active end-users
3. **Business Users:** ≥ 10,000 active annual business users
4. **Market Longevity:** Entrenched & durable position (above thresholds for at least 3 consecutive years) 

Examples of designated Gatekeepers include the Big Five: Alphabet, Amazon, Apple, Meta, and Microsoft.

## 3. Core Gatekeeper Obligations: The Dos and Don'ts (Articles 5 & 6)

The DMA replaces complex antitrust litigation with a clear legal checklist of obligations.

### 3.1. What Gatekeepers MUST Do (The "Dos"):

- **Allow Interoperability:** Gatekeepers must ensure their core software (such as instant messaging services like WhatsApp or iMessage) can interoperate basic features (texting, file sharing) with third-party messaging services.
- **Data Access for Business Users:** Gatekeepers must provide business users (e.g., merchants on Amazon) with real-time, high-quality, continuous access to the performance and marketing data generated by their business activities on the platform.
- **App Store Freedom:** Allow third-party app stores and direct sideloading of applications on their operating systems (e.g., allowing alternative marketplaces on iOS and Android).
- **Advertising Transparency:** Provide advertisers and publishers with clear, transparent metrics on the price of ads hosted on their platform.
    
### 3.2. What Gatekeepers CANNOT Do (The "Don'ts"):

- **No Self-Preferencing:** Gatekeepers are prohibited from ranking their own services or products more favorably than similar third-party services on their platform (e.g., Google cannot rank Google Shopping higher in search results simply because they own it).
- **No Unconsented Data Merging:** Gatekeepers cannot track users outside of their core platform service and merge that personal data with data collected from other services they own (e.g., Meta cannot merge Instagram data with WhatsApp data without explicit, specific GDPR-compliant consent from the user).
- **No Anti-Steering Clauses:** They cannot prevent app developers from steering customers to cheaper subscription options outside of the Gatekeeper's payment ecosystem (e.g., Apple cannot block Spotify from directing users to purchase subscriptions on Spotify's website).
- **No Pre-installed App Monopolies:** They cannot prevent users from easily uninstalling pre-installed software, browsers, or apps on their device.

## 4. Enforcement and Severe Penalties

The European Commission is the sole enforcer of the DMA, working in close collaboration with national competition authorities.

- **Fines:** Fines are designed to be devastating to deter evasion:
  - Up to 10% of the gatekeeper's total global annual turnover for the first infringement.
  - Up to 20% of the total global annual turnover for repeated infringements.

- **Behavioral Remedies (The Nuclear Option).** In cases of systematic non-compliance (infringing the rules at least 3 times in 8 years), the European Commission can impose structural remedies, including forcing the Gatekeeper to sell parts of its business (divestiture) or banning them from acquiring related tech companies.
    

## 5. Implications for Data Ethics Practitioners

1. **Consent Orchestration:** Ethicists must design explicit, un-coerced consent prompts for data-merging operations, ensuring that users who refuse data linking do not face downgraded services.
2. **Open Interoperability Design:** Ensuring that the technical protocols opened for interoperability (e.g., bridging messaging APIs) maintain zero-knowledge privacy, end-to-end encryption standards, and do not leak user metadata.
3. **Fair Market Auditing:** Assisting startups and SMEs in utilizing the real-time performance data newly liberated by the DMA to build competitive, ethical alternatives to big tech monopolies.
