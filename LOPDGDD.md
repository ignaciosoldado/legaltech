# Ley Orgánica 3/2018 (LOPDGDD) — Organic Law on Data Protection and Guarantee of Digital Rights

## 1. Executive Summary & Overview
* **Importance**: The "Ley Orgánica 3/2018, de 5 de diciembre, de Protección de Datos Personales y garantía de los derechos digitales (LOPDGDD)" is the primary national law governing data protection in Spain. It serves two distinct, crucial purposes:
* **Adaptation & Implementation of the GDPR:** It adapts Spanish national legislation to the framework established by the European General Data Protection Regulation (GDPR), clarifying ambiguities, defining specific ages of consent, and regulating national-level processing exceptions.

## 2. Key National Adjustments to the GDPR (Title I to IX)
While the GDPR is directly applicable, it allows Member States "flexibility clauses" to specify national rules. The LOPDGDD details several critical variables for Spain:

### 2.1. Age of Consent (Article 7)
* Under the GDPR, the default age for children to consent to online services is 16, though states can lower it to 13.
* The LPRDGDD sets the age of consent at 14 years old in Spain. Below 14, processing is only lawful with the consent of parents or legal guardians.

### 2.2. Processing of Deceased Persons' Data (Article 3)
* The GDPR does not apply to deceased individuals.
* The LOPDGDD allows heirs, relatives, or designated executors of a deceased person to access, rectify, or request the deletion of the deceased's personal data, unless explicitly forbidden by the deceased during their lifetime.

### 2.3. Data Protection Officer (DPO) Mandates (Article 34)
While the GDPR lists broad criteria for appointing a DPO, the LOPDGDD provides a concrete list of entities that must appoint a DPO in Spain. This includes:
* Professional associations ("Colegios Profesionales").
* Educational institutions (schools, universities, etcetera).
* Entities operating electronic communications networks or services.
* Financial institutions, credit institutions, and investment firms.
* Insurance and reinsurance companies.
* Healthcare facilities required to maintain patient clinical records.
* Entities executing advertising and market research activities involving profiling.

## 3. Title X: The Guarantee of Digital Rights (The Spanish Innovation)
Title X is a groundbreaking legislative development that addresses the ethical and social impacts of technology on human dignity.

### 3.1. Workplace Digital Rights
The LOPDGDD dramatically shaped labor relations in Spain by introducing three main workplace rights:
**1) Right to Digital Disconnection (Article 88)**
Workers have the right to disconnect from professional digital tools and communications (emails, WhatsApp, Slack, etcetera) outside of their working hours to guarantee their rest, leave, and personal/family privacy.

**2) Privacy in the Use of Digital Devices (Article 87)**
Employers may access content on company-provided devices (computers, phones) only to control work obligations or check integrity, and must establish clear, predetermined policies regarding such access.

**3) Privacy Against Video Surveillance and Geolocation (Articles 89 & 90)**
* Employers can use cameras for security and control, but never in areas dedicated to rest, recreation, or changing rooms (showers, toilets). Employees must be explicitly informed beforehand.
* GPS/Geolocation of company vehicles is permitted for labor control, but workers must be informed, and tracking outside working hours is generally prohibited.

### 3.2. Minors and Digital Media (Article 92)
* Schools and educational systems must integrate digital competence, safety, and ethics training.
* SAs, parents, and tutors can demand the immediate removal of online content that violates a minor's privacy or honor.

## 4. The AEPD
* The LOPDGDD reinforces the role of the Spanish Data Protection Agency (Agencia Española de Protección de Datos - AEPD) as the independent administrative authority overseeing compliance.
* The AEPD is tasked with investigation, enforcement, promoting public awareness, and handling cross-border data protection claims.
* It maintains a registry of DPOs and issues binding guidelines on complex technological issues (e.g., biometrics, blockchain, and cookie management).

## 5. Penalties and Sanctions (Title IX)
In addition to the GDPR's €10M/€20M scale, the LOPDGDD classifies infractions under Spanish administrative law to provide legal certainty:
* **Minor Infractions** (Prescription period: 1 year): Formal procedural failures (e.g., failure to post a compliant privacy policy, not registering a DPO when required).
* **Serious Infractions** (Prescription period: 2 years): Substantive processing failures (e.g., processing without a valid legal basis, not conducting a DPIA when mandatory, failing to notify a data breach).
* **Very Serious Infractions** (Prescription period: 3 years): Structural violations (e.g., processing sensitive data without consent or legal base, transferring data internationally without safeguards, refusing to cooperate with the AEPD).

## 6. Implications for Data Ethics Practitioners in Spain
* **Workplace Policy Auditing:** Ethicists must ensure that surveillance tools, productivity-tracking software, and HR monitoring algorithms do not infringe upon employees' Right to Digital Disconnection or general workplace privacy.
* **Child Safety Designs:** Since Spain sets the age of digital consent to 14, platforms targeting teenagers must implement age verification systems and ultra-clear "just-in-time" privacy notices adapted to minors.
* **Active DPO Collaboration:** The rigorous list of mandated DPOs in Spain means data ethicists frequently collaborate with legal compliance officers to balance operational efficiency and ethical standards.
