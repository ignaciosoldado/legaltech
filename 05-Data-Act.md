# **The EU Data Act — Regulation (EU) 2023/2854**

## **1. Executive Summary & Overview**

The European Data Act, adopted in late 2023 and fully applicable in 2025, is a groundbreaking regulation aimed at optimizing the value of data in the digital economy. While the *Data Governance Act* (DGA) sets up the trust and organizational infrastructure for data sharing, the **Data Act focuses on defining who can access and generate value from data**, especially in the context of connected devices and the Internet of Things (IoT).

The default state of the modern IoT landscape is proprietary lock-in: manufacturers of smart devices (connected cars, industrial robots, smart home appliances) often maintain exclusive control over the data generated by those devices. The Data Act dismantles this monopoly, giving consumers and businesses **unprecedented rights to access, use, and share the data they generate**.

## **2. Key Objectives & Scope**

* **Unlocking IoT Data:** Enabling users of connected devices to access data generated by their use and share it with third parties.  
* **Preventing Contractual Imbalance:** Protecting SMEs against unfair contract terms imposed by dominant actors in the data market.  
* **Public Sector Access:** Establishing pathways for public authorities to access privately held data under exceptional, emergency circumstances.  
* **Cloud Interoperability:** Making it seamless for businesses and consumers to switch between cloud, edge, and infrastructure providers without financial or technical friction.

## **3. Core Pillars & Provisions**

### **3.1. IoT Data Portability & Access (Chapter II)**

* **Access by Design:** Connected devices and related services must be designed, manufactured, and provided in such a way that data generated by their use is, **by default, easily, securely, and directly accessible** to the user (free of charge).  
* **Sharing with Third Parties:** If the user cannot access the data directly, the manufacturer (data holder) must make the data available to the user or a designated third party (e.g., an independent repair service) without delay, in a structured and commonly used format.  
* **Exclusion of Gatekeepers:** Users *cannot* authorize "gatekeeper" companies (as defined in the DMA) to receive this data, ensuring that the act benefits independent repair shops, startups, and SMEs, rather than reinforcing big tech monopolies.

### **3.2. Unfair Contract Terms (Chapter IV)**

The Data Act introduces an "unfairness test" to protect weaker parties (primarily SMEs) in data-sharing agreements.

* Any contract term concerning data access and use that is unilaterally imposed on an enterprise is **void if it is unfair**.  
* A term is considered unfair if it grossly deviates from good commercial practice, or if it inappropriately limits the rights of the party who unilaterally accepted it.

### **3.3. Business-to-Government (B2G) Data Sharing (Chapter V)**

* Private data holders must make specific data available to public sector bodies (like municipal governments or environmental agencies) if there is an **exceptional need**.  
* An "exceptional need" is defined as:  
  * Responding to a public emergency (e.g., pandemics, natural disasters).  
  * Preventing or assisting in recovery from a public emergency.  
  * Fulfilling a specific task in the public interest explicitly mandated by law, where the public body cannot otherwise obtain the data.

### **3.4. Facilitating Cloud Switching (Chapter VI)**

To combat cloud vendor lock-in, the Data Act imposes duties on cloud service providers (IaaS, PaaS, SaaS):

* Providers must remove commercial, technical, and contractual barriers that prevent customers from switching to a competing provider or porting their data.  
* **Phasing out Switching Fees:** Providers must eliminate switching fees, allowing customers to migrate data and applications at no administrative charge.

### **3.5. Smart Contracts & Interoperability (Chapter VIII)**

The Act introduces essential requirements for vendors of "Smart Contracts" used in data-sharing agreements:
* They must feature automated "kill switches" to safely terminate transactions.  
* They must be robust against manipulation and possess high-level access control.

## **4. Safeguards: Trade Secrets and IP**

One of the most intensely debated aspects of the Data Act was the protection of **Trade Secrets**.

* Manufacturers can refuse to share data if they can demonstrate that disclosure would cause serious, irreversible economic damage, leaking critical trade secrets.  
* However, they cannot use "trade secrets" as a blanket excuse to refuse data access. Refusals are subject to strict regulatory review.

## **5. Implications for Data Ethics Practitioners**

1. **Product Design Integrity:** Ethicists must work alongside hardware and IoT software engineers to ensure "Access by Design" principles are active, allowing users to inspect and extract their operational telemetry raw and unadulterated.  
2. **Sovereign Data Ecosystems:** Designing clear, fair-pricing models for data-sharing API usage, ensuring that data is made available on FRAND terms (Fair, Reasonable, and Non-Discriminatory).  
3. **Auditing Smart Contracts:** Overseeing the implementation of "kill switches" in decentralized data sharing protocols, ensuring they are governed ethically and don't introduce vulnerabilities or arbitrary censorship.
