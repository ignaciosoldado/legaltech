# General Data Protection Regulation (GDPR) — Regulation (EU) 2016/679

## 1. Overview
The General Data Protection Regulation (GDPR) is the cornerstone of modern privacy and data protection law globally. Enacted by the European Union in 2016 and fully enforceable since May 25, 2018, it replaced the outdated 1995 Data Protection Directive (Directive 95/46/EC). The GDPR was designed to harmonize data privacy laws across Europe, protect and empower all EU citizens' data privacy, and reshape the way organizations across the region approach data privacy.

## 2. Scope of Application
The GDPR has an exceptionally broad scope, intentionally designed to prevent organizations from evading compliance by relocating physical operations.

### 2.1. Material Scope (Article 2)
* Applies to the processing of personal data wholly or partly by automated means.
* Applies to non-automated processing of personal data which forms part of a filing system (or is intended to form part of one).
* Exclusions: Does not apply to purely personal or household activities ("household exemption"), activities concerning national security, or law enforcement processing (which is governed by the Law Enforcement Directive).

### 2.2. Territorial Scope (Article 3)
* Establishment Criterion: Applies to the processing of personal data in the context of the activities of an establishment of a controller or a processor in the Union, regardless of whether the processing takes place in the Union or not.
* Targeting Criterion (Extraterritorial Effect): Applies to controllers and processors not established in the Union, where the processing activities are related to:
* The offering of goods or services (even if free of charge) to data subjects in the Union.
* The monitoring of their behavior as far as their behavior takes place within the Union (e.g., behavioral tracking, profiling).

## 3. Key Definitions (Article 4)
* **Personal Data:** Any information relating to an identified or identifiable natural person ("data subject"). This includes names, ID numbers, location data, online identifiers (IP addresses, cookies), or factors specific to physical, physiological, genetic, mental, economic, cultural, or social identity.
* **Processing:** Any operation performed on personal data, whether or not by automated means, such as collection, recording, organization, structuring, storage, adaptation, retrieval, consultation, use, disclosure, dissemination, or destruction.
* **Controller:** The natural or legal person, public authority, agency, or other body which, alone or jointly with others, determines the purposes and means of the processing of personal data.
* **Processor:** A natural or legal person, public authority, agency, or other body which processes personal data on behalf of the controller.

## 4. Core Principles of Data Processing (Article 5)
Any data processing activity must satisfy the following six fundamental principles, plus the overarching principle of accountability:
* **Lawfulness, Fairness, and Transparency:** Data must be processed lawfully, fairly, and in a transparent manner in relation to the data subject.
* **Purpose Limitation:** Collected for specified, explicit, and legitimate purposes and not further processed in a manner that is incompatible with those purposes.
* **Data Minimization:** Adequate, relevant, and limited to what is necessary in relation to the purposes for which they are processed ("need-to-know" rather than "nice-to-have").
* **Accuracy:** Accurate and, where necessary, kept up to date; inaccurate data must be erased or rectified without delay.
* **Storage Limitation:** Kept in a form which permits identification of data subjects for no longer than is necessary for the purposes for which the personal data are processed.
* **Integrity and Confidentiality:** Processed in a manner that ensures appropriate security of the personal data, including protection against unauthorized or unlawful processing and against accidental loss, destruction, or damage, using appropriate technical or organizational measures.
* **Accountability:** The controller is responsible for, and must be able to demonstrate compliance with, all the principles listed above. This requires extensive documentation, audits, and proactive measures.

## 5. Legal Bases for Processing (Article 6)
Processing is only lawful if at least one of the following legal bases applies:
* **Consent:** The data subject has given clear, unambiguous, freely given, specific, and informed consent for a specific purpose.
* **Contractual Necessity:** Processing is necessary for the performance of a contract to which the data subject is party, or to take steps at the request of the data subject prior to entering into a contract.
* **Legal Obligation:** Processing is necessary for compliance with a legal obligation to which the controller is subject.
* **Vital Interests:** Processing is necessary to protect the life or vital interests of the data subject or another natural person.
* **Public Task:** Processing is necessary for the performance of a task carried out in the public interest or in the exercise of official authority vested in the controller.
* **Legitimate Interests:** Processing is necessary for the purposes of the legitimate interests pursued by the controller or by a third party, except where such interests are overridden by the fundamental rights and freedoms of the data subject (Note: This basis is not available to public authorities in the performance of their tasks).

## 6. Rights of the Data Subject (Articles 12-22)
The GDPR equips individuals with robust, enforceable rights:
* **Right to Be Informed** (Art. 13-14): Transparent information about how their data is used (usually provided via Privacy Policies).
* **Right of Access** (Art. 15): Data subjects can request a copy of their personal data and ask how it is processed (Subject Access Request - SAR).
* **Right to Rectification** (Art. 16): Right to have inaccurate or incomplete personal data corrected.
* **Right to Erasure & Right to be Forgotten** (Art. 17): Right to request deletion of data under specific circumstances (e.g., when the data is no longer necessary or consent is withdrawn).
* **Right to Restriction of Processing** (Art. 18): Right to "block" or suppress the processing of personal data in certain scenarios (e.g., while the accuracy of the data is contested).
* **Right to Data Portability** (Art. 20): Allows individuals to obtain and reuse their personal data for their own purposes across different services in a structured, commonly used, and machine-readable format.
* **Right to Object** (Art. 21): Right to object to processing based on legitimate interests, public tasks, or direct marketing (including profiling).
* **Rights Related to Automated Decision-Making & Profiling** (Art. 22): Protects individuals against decisions based solely on automated processing (including profiling) which produces legal or similarly significant effects. Data subjects have the right to obtain human intervention, express their point of view, and contest the decision.

## 7. Key Compliance Mechanisms
* **Protection by Design and by Default** (Art. 25): Privacy must be integrated into systems, services, and products from the very first stage of development, and the most privacy-friendly settings must be applied automatically (by default).
* **Protection Impact Assessments** (DPIAs - Art. 35): Mandatory for processing operations likely to result in a high risk to the rights and freedoms of natural persons, particularly when using new technologies (e.g., AI, biometric tracking, large-scale profiling).
* **Protection Officer** (DPO - Art. 37-39): Mandatory for all public authorities, and for private organizations whose core activities involve systematic, large-scale monitoring of individuals or large-scale processing of sensitive data.
* **Breach Notification** (Articles 33-34):
  * To the Supervisory Authority: Without undue delay and, where feasible, not later than 72 hours after having become aware of it, unless the breach is unlikely to result in a risk to individuals.
  * To the Data Subject: Without undue delay if the breach is likely to result in a high risk to their rights and freedoms.

## 8. Enforcement & Fines (Article 83)
The GDPR is famous for its stringent penalty framework, enforced by national Supervisory Authorities (SAs), such as the AEPD in Spain.
* **Lower Tier Fines:** Up to €10 million or 2% of the global annual turnover of the preceding financial year, whichever is higher. Applies to breaches of obligations of controllers/processors, certification bodies, etc.
* **Higher Tier Fines:** Up to €20 million or 4% of the global annual turnover of the preceding financial year, whichever is higher. Applies to breaches of core principles, legal bases, data subject rights, and international data transfer rules.

## 9. Implications for Data Ethics Practitioners
* **Algorithmic Transparency:** Art. 22 demands that automated decisions must be explainable. Ethicists must design frameworks that translate complex machine learning decisions into human-comprehensible terms.
* **Consent Architecture:** Prevent "dark patterns" (manipulative UI designs) that trick users into consenting. Consent must be as easy to withdraw as it is to give.
* **Minimization of Bias:** Active review of datasets to ensure compliance with the "accuracy" and "fairness" principles, preventing the perpetuation of systemic biases through data training.
